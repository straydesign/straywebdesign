## "It looks old" is not one of the signs

I redesign websites for a living, and I still talk people out of redesigns regularly. Plenty of plain, dated-looking sites do their job fine, and rebuilding one of those buys the owner nothing but an invoice. The reason to redesign is that the site is measurably costing you customers, and you can usually find that out yourself in an afternoon.

Here are the five signs that actually mean something, each with a test you can run today for free.

## 1. Your site is hard to use on a phone

Not "could be nicer." Hard to use. Text you pinch to read, buttons too small to tap, menus that overlap the content, a form that fights back. Most visits to a local business site happen on a phone, usually from someone standing somewhere else deciding whether to call you, and Google ranks the mobile version of your site, so a broken phone experience hurts you with every visitor and with search at the same time.

The test takes one minute. Open your site on your phone and try to do the thing a customer would do, whether that's booking, calling, or finding your hours. If it takes more than about twenty seconds or requires any zooming, you're losing people every day.

## 2. It's slow

People don't consciously time your site. They just feel the difference between it and every fast site they used that day, and they leave when something feels stuck. Slowness usually isn't a mystery either; it accumulates, one plugin and one oversized photo at a time, on sites nobody is maintaining.

To check yours, run your address through Google's free PageSpeed Insights at pagespeed.web.dev and look at the mobile score. Under about 50 means visitors are feeling the wait. While you're at it, load the site on your phone using cellular data instead of your office wifi, because that's how customers actually meet it.

## 3. You can't change anything yourself

If updating your hours means emailing somebody and paying an hourly fee for the privilege, the site has a structural problem, because sites that are annoying to update stop getting updated. The team page lists someone who left. The services list includes things you stopped doing. The holiday hours are from two holidays ago. Customers read that staleness as "is this place still open?"

A site built this decade should let you or your team edit the everyday things. My clients get that as standard, and it's the part they use most: [Andy's Pub](/work/andys) updates its own specials and [Bullfrog](/work/bullfrog) keeps its own music schedule current, no developer involved.

Here the test is a count. Go through your site and tally everything that's currently wrong, from old prices to gone employees to stale hours. More than five and the editing problem is the real problem.

## 4. It never makes the phone ring

A site that generates no calls, no form fills, and no "found you on Google" walk-ins isn't marketing, it's rent. Sometimes that's a visibility problem, but if people are reaching the site and still not contacting you, the page itself is where they're saying no. The usual reasons are mundane. There's no obvious next step on the page. The contact info is buried. The site never answers what you charge or why you're the right fit, so the visitor keeps comparison shopping. I wrote about naming that clearly in [what to look for in a web designer](/resources/blog/what-to-look-for-in-a-web-designer), because it's the exact thing a good designer should raise with you unprompted.

This one needs a month instead of a minute. Ask every new customer how they found you, and watch your form and call volume over the same stretch. If real traffic is arriving and zero leads come out, the visitors are seeing the site and deciding against you, which means fixing the page matters more than buying more visitors.

## 5. You'd pick your competitor's site

Put your homepage next to your top three competitors' homepages and show all four to somebody outside your industry. Ask one question: which business would you call? Customers do this comparison constantly, they just don't tell you about it. If your site reads as the least professional of the group, you're losing jobs before you ever get to quote them.

The exercise is the test. If you honestly wouldn't pick your own site, you have your answer.

## What to do with the results

One sign, and a targeted fix is usually enough; a slow site can often be sped up without a rebuild. Two, and it's worth an honest audit before spending anything. Three or more, and the site is working against you, and patching it piecemeal usually costs more than doing it right.

If you get there, the money side is more reasonable than the agency quotes make it look. I broke down the real three-year numbers for every route from DIY builders to agency builds in [the honest cost comparison](/resources/blog/custom-website-cost-vs-template-real-numbers). My own basic build is $500 flat plus $25 a month for hosting with the small edits included, which exists specifically so signs three and four never come back.
