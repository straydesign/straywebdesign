## What you're really asking

If you searched for a one-time payment website, you're probably not confused about technology. You're tired of renting. Wix, Squarespace, and the rest charge every month whether you touch the site or not, and the number never goes to zero.

I used to sell websites as a pure one-time payment, and I recently changed how I do it. This post is the honest version of both halves: what owning a site outright really costs, and why the math pushed me to a different answer.

## The subscription math

Builder pricing in 2026 looks like this: Squarespace runs $16 to $39 a month depending on the plan, and a Wix Business plan runs about $36 a month, both billed annually. Call it roughly $200 to $470 a year.

Over three years, the typical life of a small-business site before a redesign, that's $600 to $1,400 in rent. And at the end you own nothing. A builder site can't leave the builder. If you stop paying, the site is gone, and if you want to move, you start over from a blank page.

That last part matters more than the monthly number. The fee isn't the trap. The lock-in is.

## The questions that tell you who really owns a site

Whoever builds your site, you should know the answers to three things before money moves.

Whose account is the domain registered in? Whose account does the hosting run in? And if the person who built it disappeared tomorrow, could a different designer pick the site up next month?

If the answers all involve somebody else's accounts and a shrug, you don't own a website. You own a promise. Any designer worth hiring will answer all three in plain words before you pay a dollar, and that includes me.

## The costs that never go away

Here's what I learned selling the pure own-it-outright version: a website with no monthly fee doesn't exist, it just hides the monthly in smaller pieces.

A domain costs about $12 a year, no way around it. Hosting that allows commercial use is free in some places and about $20 a month in others, and knowing which is which is genuinely confusing. If you want to edit the site yourself with AI tools, the good ones run about $20 a month too. None of these are big numbers. Together, for an owner managing it alone, they're a part-time job's worth of accounts, renewals, and small decisions.

I watched that math while pricing my own handoff offer, and the honest conclusion was uncomfortable: for most small businesses, the do-it-all-yourself version costs about the same as having someone run it for you, and it comes with homework.

## How my version works now

So here's my current answer. The basic build is $500 flat: I design it and build it, done in about a week once your content is in. Then hosting is $25 a month, and small edits are included in that. New hours, a swapped photo, an updated price; you text me what changed and it's handled.

Bigger changes are $30 an hour, quoted before I start, so nothing lands on an invoice by surprise. The live client sites on my [home page](/#work) show the build quality; open any of them.

## When a monthly fee is actually worth paying

The test I gave people when I sold the no-monthly version still holds, and now it applies to me: ask what changed on your site last month.

A monthly fee should buy visible attention. Menus and hours kept current, photos that match what the business looks like now, pages that answer what customers actually ask. My $25 covers hosting plus exactly that kind of small, visible upkeep, and when nothing changed about your business, nothing needed to change on the site.

What a monthly fee should never buy is silence. If your site looks identical to last quarter, the invoices kept arriving, and nobody can tell you what they did, you're not paying for a service. You're funding someone's recurring revenue.

## Quick answers

**Can I still get a site with no monthly fee at all?** From some designers, yes, and the three ownership questions above are how you vet them. Budget for the domain, commercial hosting, and your own time running both.

**Why $25 instead of $10?** Because it includes the small edits. Hosting alone is cheaper; hosting plus a person who answers when your hours change is what the number covers.

**Is $500 too cheap to be real?** The pricing guides put custom builds at $500 to $10,000. Mine sits at the bottom because the offer is narrow and the overhead is one person. The build is real; so are the live sites it's judged by.
