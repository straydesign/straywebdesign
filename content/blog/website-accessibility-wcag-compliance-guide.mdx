## Most websites fail, which is your opening

WebAIM runs an annual automated audit of the top one million home pages, and the 2025 edition found detectable accessibility failures on 94.8% of them, averaging about fifty errors per page. The two most common problems are mundane: text without enough contrast to read comfortably, and images with no alternative text for screen readers.

Those failures aren't obscure technicalities. Low-contrast text means someone with aging eyes can't read your phone number. Missing alt text means a screen reader user has no idea what your hero image shows. A form input without a label means a blind visitor cannot fill out your contact form at all, so the lead you paid to attract physically cannot reach you.

I'd rather frame that as an opportunity than a threat. The bar is embarrassingly low, so a site that simply works for everyone stands out, and it picks up the customers the broken sites turn away.

## The legal side, without the scare tactics

Accessibility lawsuits against business websites are real and numerous; UsableNet, which tracks these filings, counted over four thousand in 2024, and most defendants are ordinary small and mid-sized companies rather than household names. Courts have generally treated WCAG 2.1 Level AA, the W3C's published guideline set, as the working standard. The EU added its own requirements in 2025 for businesses selling to European consumers.

That's the honest extent of what I'll say about legal risk, because I design websites and I'm not a lawyer. The practical takeaway is simply that an accessible build removes a category of exposure while making the site better at its actual job.

## The overlay widgets don't work

You will get sales emails offering "instant compliance" from a JavaScript widget for a monthly fee. Be skeptical. In January 2025 the FTC fined accessiBe, the biggest vendor in that market, one million dollars over deceptive compliance claims. UsableNet's tracking has repeatedly found lawsuits filed against sites that were running overlays when they were sued, with the widgets themselves sometimes cited as barriers because they interfere with the assistive technology people actually use.

The deeper problem is that an overlay doesn't change your site's code, and the code is where the failures live. Accessibility is a property of how a site is built, which is why it can't be rented for $50 a month.

## What an accessible site actually means

WCAG organizes everything under four plain-English principles. Perceivable means people can take in your content: images carry descriptive alt text, videos carry captions, and body text keeps at least a 4.5-to-1 contrast ratio against its background. Operable means everything works without a mouse, so a visitor using only a keyboard can reach every menu, link, and form field without getting trapped. Understandable means forms have visible labels and error messages that say what went wrong in human words. Robust means the underlying code is clean semantic HTML that assistive technology can parse, with a real heading hierarchy instead of styled paragraphs.

None of that constrains how a site looks. It constrains how carelessly a site can be built, which is a different thing.

## Why it pays even if nobody ever sues anyone

The CDC's long-standing estimate is that about one in four US adults lives with some disability. The UK's Click-Away Pound survey found that roughly two-thirds of disabled users who hit a barrier simply leave without complaining, which means you'll never see the loss in a feedback form, only in the absence of calls.

And the same work helps everyone else. Real heading structure is what search engines parse to understand your pages. Higher contrast helps anyone reading a phone in sunlight. Labeled forms get abandoned less by everybody. Keyboard support helps power users. In my experience the accessibility pass and the general quality pass are mostly the same pass.

## How to check your own site today

Run your homepage through a free automated scanner like WAVE (wave.webaim.org) or the axe browser extension, the same engine most professional auditors use. Then do two manual checks the scanners can't: put your mouse away and try to reach your contact form using only the Tab and Enter keys, and squint at your site from arm's length to see whether the text survives. Most local business sites I check fail within the first minute of this, usually on contrast and form labels.

If your site fails badly, the question becomes whether to patch or rebuild, and [the redesign-signs post](/resources/blog/five-signs-you-need-a-website-redesign) covers how I'd decide. If you're hiring someone, add accessibility to the questions in [the web designer buying guide](/resources/blog/what-to-look-for-in-a-web-designer), and expect a plain-words answer rather than a widget pitch.

For my own builds, accessibility checks run before launch as part of the standard process rather than as a paid add-on, because retrofitting it later is the expensive version. It's the same reason my builds are fast: the cheapest time to care is while the site is being made.
