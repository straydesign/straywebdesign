## Both sticker prices mislead

A template site looks cheap next to a custom quote, and a custom quote looks like a ripoff next to a $49 theme. Both impressions fall apart once you count three years instead of day one, which is roughly how long a site runs before most businesses want it redone anyway.

I build websites for a living, so I have a horse in this race. I'll keep the numbers honest anyway, including the cases where a template is genuinely the right call. If you want the general buying guide first, that's in [what to look for in a web designer](/resources/blog/what-to-look-for-in-a-web-designer).

## What the template route really costs

If you build it yourself on a website builder, the pricing is public. As of mid-2026, Wix's main business plan runs $36 a month and Squarespace runs $16 to $39 depending on the plan. Call it $600 to $1,300 over three years, plus your domain at roughly $12 to $15 a year. I walked through those numbers, and what you do and don't own at the end, in [the ownership breakdown](/resources/blog/one-time-payment-website-own-it-outright).

The WordPress version looks cheaper on paper. A theme is free to about $80, shared hosting is a few dollars a month, and the domain is the same $12 to $15. The costs that surprise people come later, and they're not exotic: premium plugins that renew every year, a hosting upgrade once the cheap tier gets slow, and paying someone by the hour when an update breaks something and the person who set it up is gone. None of that is guaranteed to happen to you. All of it is common enough that you should budget as if some of it will.

There's also the cost that never shows up on an invoice, which is your own time. A DIY site makes you the webmaster. Some owners like that job. Most of the ones I talk to stopped enjoying it about a month in.

## What the hired route really costs

Agencies quoting small business sites in 2026 mostly land between $3,000 and $15,000, with the low end for a straightforward marketing site and the high end for booking systems, portals, or online stores. That range is real, and so is what stands behind it at a good agency: strategy, custom design, development, and project management as separate people's jobs.

A solo designer does the same class of work without the overhead, which is why solo quotes land so much lower. Mine is $500 flat for the basic build, then $25 a month for hosting with small edits included. Hours change, a photo swaps, a price updates: send it over, it's handled. Bigger changes are $30 an hour, quoted before I start. Over three years that's $1,400 all in, and you can see exactly what the build buys on the [Andy's Pub](/work/andys) and [Bullfrog](/work/bullfrog) case studies, both live client sites you can open right now.

## The three-year table

Rough totals for a typical small business site, using the public numbers above:

- Website builder, DIY: $650 to $1,350, plus your time every month
- WordPress, DIY with occasional hired help: anywhere from a few hundred dollars to a few thousand, depending on how the plugin lottery treats you
- My build: $1,400, maintenance included
- Agency build: $3,000 to $15,000 up front, plus whatever their care plan costs monthly

The spread between those options is not really about code quality. It's about who does the ongoing work, how much of it there is, and whether the price of that work is fixed or open-ended.

## When a template is the right call

Templates make sense more often than designers admit. If you're testing a business idea and might fold it in six months, use a builder. If your work is entirely referral and the site is a business card nobody reads, use a builder. If the budget honestly stops at a few hundred dollars, a clean template you keep current beats a custom site you can't afford.

The template stops making sense when customers actually use the site to decide. If people find you on Google, compare you against the shop across town, and choose whether to call based on what they see, then the site is doing sales work, and the questions that matter change. How fast does it load on a phone? Does it say what you charge? Can a stranger tell why you're the right fit in ten seconds? A template can pass those tests, but somebody has to do the work, and that somebody is you.

## How to decide

Count three years, not day one. Price the builder plans, get two or three real quotes, and ask every designer the same thing: what does it cost when I need something changed? The answer to that last question predicts your next three years better than the sticker price does.
