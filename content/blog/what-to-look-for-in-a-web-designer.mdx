## Hiring a web designer is a business decision

You're not buying a website. You're buying the thing that represents your business to every customer who checks you out before calling, and it'll do that job for years. The difference between a good hire and a bad one shows up in leads and phone calls, long after everyone has stopped admiring the screenshots.

Most businesses still pick a designer the way they'd pick a restaurant: gut feeling, a glance at a portfolio, whoever's cheapest. I build websites for a living, so this is the buying guide from the inside, including the parts that make my own industry look bad.

## The red flags

### No live sites to show you

A designer who can only show screenshots or mockups is hiding something. Screenshots can't show load times, how the site behaves on a phone, or whether the thing still works two years after launch. Live sites can.

Ask for the addresses of real sites they've built, then open each one on your phone. Slow to load, broken menus, a design that falls apart on a small screen: whatever you find is what you'll get. If they have no live work at all, they may just be new, which isn't disqualifying, but you should know that's what you're buying.

### Unclear pricing

Web design pricing varies wildly because overhead varies wildly. Agencies commonly quote $3,000 to $15,000 for a small business site, and a solo designer without an office or account managers can do the same class of work for far less. The number tells you less than what stands behind it.

The patterns that should worry you: a price with no visible work behind it, no clear definition of what's included, and hourly billing with no estimate, which is a blank check. Whatever the price, get the scope in writing.

For what it's worth, my own basic build is $500 flat plus $25 a month for hosting with small edits included, and the [live client sites on my home page](/#work) are what that buys. The wider market numbers are in [the cost comparison guide](/resources/blog/custom-website-cost-vs-template-real-numbers).

### Template work at custom prices

Templates aren't inherently evil. The problem is a designer charging custom prices for template work, or a template dragging in baggage you didn't sign up for: slow pages, plugins that need babysitting, a layout that looks like every other site built from the same kit.

The useful question isn't "do you use templates?" It's "what does your approach mean for speed and upkeep?" A designer building custom on a lightweight foundation and one rearranging sections in a bloated theme will both say they "customize." The first one can explain the difference.

### No mention of measurement

Your site exists to make the phone ring, fill the booking form, or bring people through the door. A designer who never mentions measuring any of that is planning to hand you something pretty and hope. At minimum they should set up analytics, track the actions that matter (calls, form fills, clicks to order), and show you where to see the numbers. If the whole conversation is colors and fonts, the site might work or might not, and nobody will ever know.

### "We can make it show up on Google later"

How a site shows up on Google is decided while it's being built: the page titles, the headings, how fast it loads, the structured data that tells Google what your business is, the way pages link to each other. Bolting that on afterward is like adding a foundation after the house is up.

One question sorts this out fast: "how do you handle structured data?" A designer building sites that show up in modern search results will have an answer in plain words. A blank stare means that part of the job isn't being done.

## The green flags

### They ask about your business before your taste

The first conversation should include questions like: who is the customer, where do your leads come from now, and what's the one action the site needs to drive? Bookings, calls, form fills, foot traffic. A designer who starts there is building a tool. One who opens with "what colors do you like?" is starting with aesthetics and hoping the business part works out.

### They can explain their choices

Whatever they build on, they should be able to tell you why, in words you understand, including the trade-offs. Someone who can't explain their own stack chose it by default. You don't need to care about the technology, but you do need the person who picked it to have had a reason.

### Updates don't all run through them

Ask what happens when your hours change or a menu item goes up a dollar. The good answers are either "your team edits that yourselves" or "send it over, it's included." My clients get both kinds: [Andy's Pub](/work/andys) and [Bullfrog](/work/bullfrog) edit their own menus and events through a system I set up, and my hosting includes the small stuff when owners would rather just text me the change. The bad answer is a designer who bills an hour every time a photo needs swapping.

### There's a plan for after launch

Launch day is the beginning. Somebody has to keep the site online and current, and you should know who that is and what it costs before you sign anything. A designer who builds it and disappears is leaving you with an asset that quietly rots. Ask what happens after launch and get the answer in writing.

## The seven questions to ask before you hire

1. **"Can I see live sites you've built and still look after?"** Open them on your phone and judge for yourself.

2. **"What do you build on, and why?"** Any clear, honest answer passes. A vague one doesn't.

3. **"How will we know the site is working?"** They should name specific numbers they'd watch, like calls, form fills, and where the site shows up on Google.

4. **"What happens when I need something changed?"** Small changes should be cheap or included, and you should know which before you sign.

5. **"How do you handle structured data?"** The plain-words test for whether the Google side of the job is real.

6. **"What's included after launch?"** Hosting and upkeep, plus who to call when something breaks. In writing.

7. **"What's the total cost, and what's not included?"** Fixed scope, fixed price, no surprises.

## What a good hire looks like

A good web designer builds you something measurable and maintainable that earns back what it cost, then keeps earning. The red flags are easy to spot once you know them. The green flags are rarer, and they mostly come down to one thing: the designer talks about your business first and their design second.

Take the time to ask the seven questions. The person who answers them well is the one to hire, whatever their price is.
