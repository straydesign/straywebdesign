## Why speed is worth your attention

Nobody times your website with a stopwatch. Visitors just feel the difference between your site and every fast site they used that day, and they quietly leave when something feels stuck. Google also folds its Core Web Vitals metrics into ranking, so a slow site loses twice: once with the visitor, once with the search results that decide whether the visitor arrives at all.

The good news is that slow sites are rarely slow for exotic reasons. Speed problems accumulate from a handful of ordinary causes, and the fixes below are the ones I actually run on every build. If someone else maintains your site, this post is written plainly enough to hand to them as a checklist.

## Images: the biggest win

Images are usually the heaviest thing on a business website, which makes them the best place to start.

Modern formats do a lot of the work by themselves. WebP files come out roughly a quarter to a third smaller than the same-quality JPEG, and AVIF pushes further still on large photography. Browser support for both is broad enough in 2026 that there's little reason to serve plain JPEG. The `<picture>` element handles the fallback chain:

```html
<picture>
  <source srcset="hero.avif" type="image/avif" />
  <source srcset="hero.webp" type="image/webp" />
  <img src="hero.jpg" alt="Description" width="1200" height="600" />
</picture>
```

Just as important: don't send a desktop-sized image to a phone. The `srcset` and `sizes` attributes let the browser pick an appropriately sized file, which can cut the mobile image payload by more than half on photo-heavy pages.

Everything below the fold gets `loading="lazy"` so the browser defers it until the visitor scrolls near. The one exception is your largest above-the-fold element, usually the hero image. Never lazy-load that one; preload it instead:

```html
<link rel="preload" as="image" href="hero.avif" type="image/avif" />
```

If the site runs on Next.js, which is what I build on, the built-in image component does the format negotiation and resizing automatically. The discipline is mostly in not overriding it.

## JavaScript: ship less of it

A kilobyte of JavaScript costs more than a kilobyte of image, because a script has to be downloaded and then run before the page feels responsive. The fixes are unglamorous. Remove libraries the site no longer uses. Let the framework split code by route so the homepage doesn't download the booking page's logic. Defer anything heavy until it's actually needed on screen.

Watch for the quiet offenders here: the jQuery include left over from an old animation, the chat widget nobody staffs, the analytics script nobody reads. Each one taxes every single page view.

## Hosting and caching: serve from nearby

Most pages on a business site don't change between visits, so they shouldn't be rebuilt for every visitor. I pre-render pages at build time and serve them from an edge network, meaning static files delivered from a server physically near the visitor rather than a single origin far away. Platforms like Vercel and Cloudflare make this the default rather than an engineering project, and it's a large part of why a well-built site feels instant even to a first-time visitor with a cold cache.

If your site sits on cheap shared hosting and feels sluggish before any page even starts rendering, the hosting layer is usually the culprit rather than anything in your content.

## Core Web Vitals: the numbers Google actually checks

Google publishes its three thresholds, and they're the scoreboard I build against.

Largest Contentful Paint measures how fast the main content appears, and the passing mark is under 2.5 seconds. It's won or lost on image handling and render-blocking resources. Interaction to Next Paint measures how fast the page responds when someone taps, with a threshold of 200 milliseconds, and it's almost entirely a question of JavaScript weight. Cumulative Layout Shift measures how much the page jumps around while loading, with a passing score under 0.1, and the cure is giving every image explicit dimensions plus reserved space for anything that loads late.

On my own builds I target comfortable margins under all three thresholds rather than scraping past them. You can check any site's numbers, including mine or your current designer's, with Google's free PageSpeed Insights at pagespeed.web.dev. That's also the honest way to evaluate a designer's portfolio: run their live work through it.

## Fonts: small files, loaded early

Web fonts silently block text from rendering while they download. Three habits keep them cheap: load only the character sets you use (Google Fonts subsets automatically; self-hosted fonts need a tool like glyphhanger), preload the one or two files the first screen needs, and prefer a single variable font file over separate uploads for every weight.

```html
<link rel="preload" as="font" href="/fonts/heading.woff2" type="font/woff2" crossorigin />
```

## Third-party scripts: audit ruthlessly

Tag managers, pixels, heat maps, chat bubbles. Each adds weight and main-thread work, and they stack. The audit question for every one of them: does anyone at your business actually look at this data or answer this widget? If not, removing it is the cheapest speed upgrade available. The ones that survive should wait until the page is interactive before they load.

## The pre-launch checklist

- Images in modern formats, correctly sized, lazy-loaded below the fold, with the hero preloaded
- No render-blocking scripts, unused libraries, or unaudited third-party tags
- Fonts subsetted and preloaded
- Pages pre-rendered and served from an edge network
- All three Core Web Vitals passing on mobile in PageSpeed Insights

None of this requires a big retainer, it requires someone caring at build time.
